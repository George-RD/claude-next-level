# Hormozi Guarantee Naming Framework -- Research Document

**Source**: Alex Hormozi, *$100M Offers: How To Make Offers So Good People Feel Stupid Saying No* (Chapter 15: Guarantees)
**Purpose**: Comprehensive research to inform a workshop plugin feature spec
**Date**: 2026-03-19

---

## 1. Core Philosophy: Risk Reversal

Hormozi's central thesis on guarantees:

> "The most significant objection to any sale of a good or service is the risk that it will not work for a prospect."

> "Reversing risk is the number one way to increase the conversion of an offer."

Guarantees work by shifting risk from the customer to the business. Jason Fladlien (cited by Hormozi) observed that conversions can increase **2-4x** simply by changing the quality of the guarantee. Experienced marketers spend as much time crafting their guarantees as the deliverables themselves.

### Connection to the Value Equation

Guarantees directly impact Hormozi's Value Equation:

```
Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)
```

Guarantees increase the **Perceived Likelihood of Achievement** (numerator) and reduce the perceived **Effort & Sacrifice** (denominator) by eliminating downside risk. The word "perceived" is critical -- what a prospect *believes* to be true is what counts.

### The Profitability Math

Even with higher refund rates, guarantees net more sales:

- Without guarantee: 100 sales, 5 refunds (5%) = **95 net sales**
- With guarantee: 130 sales, 13 refunds (10%) = **117 net sales**
- Result: **23% net increase** despite a doubled refund rate

For guarantees to reduce profit, increased refunds would need to completely offset sales gains -- an unlikely scenario for businesses with solid products.

## 2. The Four Guarantee Types

Hormozi identifies four overarching categories, with **13 specific structures** derived from two foundational types (unconditional and conditional).

### Type 1: Unconditional Guarantees

The strongest guarantee type. Functions as a trial where customers pay first, then evaluate.

| # | Structure | Description |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | **"No Questions Asked" Refund** | Simple full refund with no conditions. Risky as it holds you fully accountable. |
| 2 | **Satisfaction-Based Refund** | Triggers when the prospect feels unsatisfied with the service. |

**Best for**: Low-ticket B2C situations where the friction of requesting a refund naturally deters most claims.

**Example script**: "Best case you get the body of your dreams and we give you all your money towards staying with us to hit your long-term goal. Worst case you tell me I suck, I write you a check, and you get six weeks of free training."

**Warning**: Becomes very risky with higher costs of fulfillment because you eat the cost of the refund AND the cost of fulfilling.

### Type 2: Conditional Guarantees

Include specific "terms and conditions" that the customer must meet. Typically outperform traditional money-back guarantees.

**The Conditional Guarantee Formula**:

```
"If you do not get [X result] in [Y time period], we will [Z]."
```

| # | Structure | Description |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 3 | **Outsized Refund** | Additional money back (e.g., 2x or 3x) attached to the customer doing the work to qualify |
| 4 | **Service Guarantee** | Continue providing work free of charge until X result is achieved |
| 5 | **Modified Service** | Grant another period Y of service or access free of charge (Y should be >= 2x the original duration) |
| 6 | **Credit-Based** | Provide a refund in the form of a credit toward your other offers |
| 7 | **Personal Service** | Work with the client one-on-one for free until X result is achieved |
| 8 | **Hotel + Airfare Perks** | Reimburse hotel and airfare costs if they received no value |
| 9 | **Wage-Payment** | Pay their hourly rate if they don't get value from your session |
| 10 | **Release of Service** | Cancel the contract free of charge if they stop getting value |
| 11 | **Delayed Second Payment** | Stop the 2nd payment until the first outcome is reached |
| 12 | **First Outcome** | Cover ancillary costs until they reach their first outcome |

**Best for**: Higher-ticket, B2B-oriented offers where specificity builds credibility.

**Key insight**: Tying conditions to customer actions protects you while future-pacing the prospect into believing the outcome. If a customer must do steps 1, 2, and 3 to qualify, and doing those steps properly would get results anyway, the guarantee becomes self-fulfilling.

**Example**: "You will get 20 clients in your first 30 days, or we give you your money back plus your advertising dollars spent with us."

### Type 3: Anti-Guarantee

Explicitly states "all sales are final" with a creative reason why.

| # | Structure | Description |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 13 | **Anti-Guarantee** | A non-guarantee that frames finality as a feature, not a bug |

**Works well for**:

- High-ticket products/services requiring significant customization or effort
- Situations where revealing proprietary processes is the product
- Consumable or highly devalued products

**Example script**: "We are going to show you our proprietary process that we are using right now to generate leads in our business. Our funnels, ads, and metrics. We're going to be exposing the inner workings of our business, as a result, all sales are final."

**Alternative framing**: "If you're the type of customer who needs a guarantee before taking a jump, then you are not the type of person we want to work with. We want motivated self-starters who can follow instructions and are not looking for a way out before they even begin. If you are not serious, don't buy it. But if you are, boy are you going to make a killing."

**Psychology**: The anti-guarantee acts as a filter for serious buyers. It pre-qualifies prospects by self-selecting for commitment.

### Type 4: Implied Guarantees

Performance-based offers where the guarantee is implicit -- if the seller doesn't perform, they don't get paid. Based on trust and transparency.

| Structure | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Performance** | Pay $X per sale, show, or milestone |
| **Revenue-Share** | Pay X% of top-line revenue or X% of revenue growth |
| **Profit-Share** | Pay X% of profit or X% of gross profit |
| **Ratchets** | Conditional payment tiers (10% if over X, 20% if over Y, 30% if over Z) |
| **Bonuses/Triggers** | Pay X when Y event occurs |

**Key concept**: These tie the seller's compensation directly to performance, creating full alignment between seller and customer success. "If I don't perform, I don't get paid."

**Hormozi's preference**: He recommends "selling service-based guarantees or setting up performance partnerships" as his favorite approach. This makes all sales final (eliminating refund fear) while keeping the business committed to customer results.

## 3. Named Guarantees: The Naming Concept

### What Makes a Named Guarantee Different

A named guarantee transforms a generic promise ("30-day money-back guarantee") into a **memorable, branded commitment** that stands out and becomes part of the offer's identity. Rather than vanilla terms like "satisfaction guarantee," Hormozi advocates creating vivid, creative names that:

1. **Capture attention** through unexpected or dramatic imagery
2. **Become talkable** -- customers remember and share them
3. **Signal confidence** -- a creative name implies the business is so confident they can be playful about it
4. **Differentiate** the offer from competitors using generic guarantees

### Examples of Named Guarantees from the Book

| Named Guarantee | What It Actually Is | Why It Works |
|----------------|---------------------|--------------|
| **"Club a Baby Seal Guarantee"** | Unconditional 30-day refund | Vivid, shocking imagery makes it unforgettable. "After 30 days of using our services, if you wouldn't club a baby seal to stay on as a customer, you don't have to pay a penny." |
| **"Shark Infested Waters Guarantee"** | Unconditional 30-day refund | "In 30 days, if you wouldn't jump into shark infested waters to get our product back, we will return every dollar you paid." |
| **"Love It or Leave It Guarantee"** | Unconditional satisfaction-based | Clean, simple, communicates zero-risk trial. |
| **"Triple Your Money Back"** | Conditional outsized refund (3x) | Boldness signals extreme confidence. Conditional on customer completing the work. |

### The Guarantee Naming Process

Hormozi's approach to creating named guarantees:

1. **Identify prospect fears**: Ask "What do they not want to have happen if they pay you? What are they most afraid of?" Consider:
   - Financial loss (the obvious one)
   - Wasted time
   - Social embarrassment
   - Missed opportunity cost
   - Emotional investment lost

2. **Reverse the fears into a guarantee**: Each fear becomes a promise. If they fear wasting time, guarantee their time. If they fear losing money, guarantee more than their money back.

3. **Make it "better than money back"**: Match the psychological investment with an equal or higher perceived commitment. If someone pays $5,000, a simple refund doesn't fully reverse the risk because they also invested time, energy, and hope.

4. **Give it a creative name**: Use vivid, unexpected imagery or language that:
   - Would sound absurd in a competitor's marketing
   - Creates a mental picture
   - Implies extreme confidence
   - Is easy to remember and repeat

5. **Spell out the conditional terms clearly**: For conditional guarantees, the name should hint at the boldness while the terms create a self-fulfilling prophecy.

## 4. Guarantee Stacking

### The Concept

Like bonuses, guarantees can be stacked -- combining multiple guarantee types to virtually eliminate risk for the prospect. Each guarantee addresses a different fear or concern.

### Stacking Patterns

**Pattern 1: Unconditional + Conditional**

- Layer 1: Unconditional 30-day no-questions-asked guarantee
- Layer 2: Conditional triple-your-money-back 90-day guarantee
- Effect: Short-term safety net + long-term bold commitment

**Pattern 2: Multiple Conditional Guarantees (Sequential Outcomes)**

- Layer 1: "You'll make $10,000 by 60 days"
- Layer 2: "You'll make $30,000 by 90 days"
- Condition: "As long as you do steps 1, 2, and 3"
- Effect: Future-paces the prospect into specific milestones, making success feel inevitable

**Pattern 3: Multi-Dimension Guarantee Stack**

- Layer 1: Money-back guarantee for results
- Layer 2: Satisfaction guarantee for service quality
- Layer 3: Convenience guarantee for scheduling flexibility
- Effect: Addresses financial, emotional, and practical fears separately

### Psychology of Stacking

Stacking works because it:

1. **Demonstrates seriousness** about the customer's outcome
2. **Future-paces** the prospect into outcomes they now believe are far more likely (since you are deliberately spelling them out with timelines)
3. **Addresses multiple objection types** simultaneously -- a single guarantee only handles one fear
4. **Creates a "net" of safety** that makes the purchase feel truly risk-free

## 5. Risk Reversal Psychology

### Why Named Guarantees Work Better Than Generic Ones

1. **Attention and memorability**: "30-day money-back guarantee" is invisible -- every competitor says it. "The Club a Baby Seal Guarantee" stops you in your tracks.

2. **Confidence signaling**: A business willing to name their guarantee creatively signals they rarely have to honor it. The playfulness implies certainty of delivery.

3. **Skin in the game** (Nassim Taleb concept): When you risk refunds, repairs, or additional free work, you demonstrate alignment with the customer's success. This creates trust because the customer knows you are incentivized to deliver.

4. **Prospect self-persuasion**: The vivid language of a named guarantee forces the prospect to imagine the positive outcome. "If you wouldn't club a baby seal to stay" makes them imagine being so satisfied they would do something extreme -- which pre-programs the positive experience.

5. **Social proof amplifier**: Named guarantees become talking points. Customers tell friends, "They have this crazy guarantee where..."

### The "Better Than Money Back" Principle

Hormozi's key insight: money-back is not enough for high-commitment purchases because the customer also invested:

- **Time** (hours spent in the program/service)
- **Opportunity cost** (what else they could have done)
- **Emotional energy** (hope, vulnerability, identity)
- **Outside costs** (travel, childcare, materials)

"Better than money back" guarantees address these non-financial costs. Examples:

- Pay for a competitor's service if yours fails
- Reimburse hotel and airfare
- Pay their hourly wage for time spent
- Continue working for free until results achieved

## 6. Strategic Guidance: When to Use Which Type

### Ticket Price / Market Matrix

| Scenario | Recommended Guarantee Type |
|----------|---------------------------|
| Low-ticket B2C | Unconditional (broad). Friction of requesting refund naturally deters most claims. |
| Mid-ticket B2C/B2B | Conditional with specific outcomes and timelines. |
| High-ticket B2B | Specific conditional guarantees, implied/performance-based, or anti-guarantees. |
| Proprietary/IP-heavy | Anti-guarantee with creative reasoning. |
| Consulting/Agency | Service guarantee ("we work until you get results") or performance partnership (rev-share). |
| Commoditized market | Bold named unconditional to differentiate. |

### Hormozi's Personal Preference

He favors **service-based guarantees** and **performance partnerships** because they:

- Make all sales final (no refund fear)
- Keep the business committed to customer results
- Create full alignment between seller and customer
- Eliminate the "guarantee shoppers" problem

## 7. Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: No Guarantee at All

The biggest mistake. Even an anti-guarantee is better than silence, because silence leaves the risk ambiguously on the customer.

### Mistake 2: Weak Generic Language

"Satisfaction guaranteed" and "30-day money-back guarantee" are invisible. They blend in with every competitor and fail to capture attention or build differentiation.

### Mistake 3: Unconditional on High-Ticket with High Fulfillment Cost

If your cost of fulfillment is high, an unconditional guarantee means you eat both the refund AND the fulfillment cost. Use conditional or anti-guarantees instead.

### Mistake 4: Attracting "Guarantee Shoppers"
>
> "A person who only buys because of a guarantee is a person who may not be willing to put in the work necessary to see success."

People who buy primarily because of the guarantee tend to be worse clients. Conditional guarantees solve this by tying the guarantee to effort.

### Mistake 5: Not Tying Conditions to Required Actions

Conditional guarantees should require actions that would produce success anyway. If the customer must complete steps 1, 2, and 3 (which are the steps that produce results), then 100% compliance = success, and the guarantee is rarely triggered.

### Mistake 6: Discounting Instead of Guaranteeing

Never discount your main offer. Discounting teaches customers that prices are negotiable. Instead, add guarantees and bonuses to increase perceived value.

### Mistake 7: Treating the Guarantee as an Afterthought

Hormozi emphasizes that the guarantee deserves as much creative energy as the deliverables. It is a core component of the offer, not a footnote.

### Mistake 8: Not Stating the Guarantee Boldly
>
> "You must always hit your guarantee hard, even without one. State it boldly and explain why."

Whether you have a guarantee or an anti-guarantee, present it with conviction and confidence, not apologetically.

## 8. The MAGIC Naming Formula (for Offers, Applicable to Guarantees)

While the MAGIC formula is Hormozi's framework for naming *offers* (not guarantees specifically), its principles can be adapted for guarantee naming:

| Component | Meaning | Offer Example | Guarantee Adaptation |
|-----------|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| **M** - Magnet | Magnetic reason why / attention hook | "Winter Solstice Sale" | Use vivid, attention-grabbing imagery ("Club a Baby Seal") |
| **A** - Avatar | Announce who it's for | "Soccer Moms of Waterville" | Specify who the guarantee protects |
| **G** - Goal | State the dream outcome | "Double Your Clients" | Name the guaranteed result |
| **I** - Interval | Define the timeframe | "in 6 Months" | Specify the guarantee window |
| **C** - Container | Wrap in a category word | "Accelerator", "Blueprint" | Use a guarantee-type word ("Guarantee", "Promise", "Pact") |

**Rule**: Use 3-5 components to keep the name succinct and memorable.

**Full offer name examples**:

- "10K Club: High Ticket Client Accelerator"
- "Luxury Valentine's Escape for Newlyweds"
- "72-Hour Black Friday Cyber Blitz"

**Guarantee naming adaptation examples** (synthesized from framework):

- "[Vivid Image] + [Result] + Guarantee" -- e.g., "The Shark-Proof 20-Client Guarantee"
- "[Bold Commitment] + [Timeframe] + Promise" -- e.g., "The Triple-Down 90-Day Promise"
- "[Anti-fear] + Guarantee" -- e.g., "The No-Hostage Guarantee" (you won't feel trapped)

## 9. The Five Offer Enhancers (Context for Guarantees)

Guarantees are one of five "enhancers" that Hormozi recommends layering onto the core offer:

1. **Scarcity** -- Limited quantity
2. **Urgency** -- Limited time
3. **Bonuses** -- Added value components
4. **Guarantees** -- Risk reversal
5. **Naming** -- Creative naming via MAGIC formula

These work together. A named guarantee that uses scarcity ("Only 10 spots qualify for the Triple-Down Guarantee") or urgency ("This guarantee expires at midnight") compounds the effect.

## 10. Implementation Checklist (Synthesized from Research)

### Creating a Named Guarantee -- Step by Step

1. **List prospect fears**: What are they most afraid of if they buy? (Financial loss, time waste, embarrassment, being "stuck", not getting results)

2. **Choose guarantee type(s)**: Based on ticket price, market, and fulfillment cost, select from the 13 structures.

3. **Write the conditional statement**: "If you do not get [X result] in [Y time period], we will [Z]."

4. **Make it "better than money back"**: Can you add time reimbursement, cover outside costs, or offer outsized refunds?

5. **Name it**: Apply MAGIC-style naming -- use vivid imagery, specify the result, include a timeframe, wrap in a container word.

6. **Consider stacking**: Can you combine an unconditional short-term guarantee with a conditional long-term one?

7. **Stress-test**: Does your guarantee attract the right customers? Does it tie conditions to actions that produce results?

8. **Present boldly**: Script the guarantee presentation for sales conversations. State it with conviction.
